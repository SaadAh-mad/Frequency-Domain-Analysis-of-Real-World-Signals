Observations

Clap

The clap signal exhibits a relatively high spectral centroid (~6099 Hz) and the highest zero-crossing rate among the analyzed signals (0.22).
A clap is a very short impulsive sound that contains energy distributed across a wide range of frequencies. Because the signal changes rapidly in time, it produces many zero crossings and strong high-frequency components, resulting in a large spectral centroid.

Bird Sounds

The bird signal shows a dominant frequency around 4325 Hz and a moderately high spectral centroid (~4131 Hz).
Bird calls often contain sharp tonal components in the higher frequency range, which explains the high dominant frequency and elevated centroid. The zero-crossing rate (0.16) is lower than the clap but still relatively high due to rapid oscillations in the waveform.

Traffic Noise

Traffic noise has a dominant frequency around 60 Hz and a much lower spectral centroid (~1755 Hz) compared to the other signals.
This indicates that traffic sound contains more low-frequency energy generated by engines, road vibrations, and vehicle movement. The very low zero-crossing rate (~0.03) reflects slower waveform variations and smoother fluctuations compared to impulsive sounds like a clap.


Overall, the analyzed signals show clear differences in their spectral characteristics.
Impulsive sounds such as claps produce broadband frequency content and high zero-crossing rates, while continuous environmental sounds such as traffic exhibit stronger low-frequency components and lower zero-crossing rates. Bird calls tend to concentrate energy in higher frequency bands, resulting in higher dominant frequencies and spectral centroids.

These differences demonstrate how frequency-domain features can be used to characterize and distinguish different real-world signals.
